# Tour Guide Notes: River Valley Cruise (Signature Loop)

This document provides historical context, fascinating tidbits, and engaging anecdotes for each major stop on the River Valley Cruise. Use these to enrich the rider experience and bring Edmonton's history to life.

### Land Acknowledgement
We acknowledge that the land we travel on is Treaty 6 territory, a traditional gathering place for diverse Indigenous peoples including the Cree, Dene, Saulteaux, Blackfoot, Nakota Sioux, and Métis. We respect and honor their histories, languages, and cultures, and their enduring connection to this land.

---

## 1. Meeting Point: Pedego Electric Bikes
*   **The Partnership:** Highlight our collaboration with Pedego. Mention that e-bikes "level the playing field," allowing groups of different fitness levels to stay together and enjoy the hills of the River Valley without breaking a sweat.
*   **Safety First:** Ensure everyone is comfortable with the throttle and pedal-assist before heading out.
*   **The Hub:** We are starting in Old Strathcona, which was its own city until 1912. This neighborhood was the "End of Steel" for the Canadian Pacific Railway, making it the primary gateway for settlers arriving from the south. Starting here connects us directly to the era when Edmonton was two rival cities.

## 2. Mill Creek Ravine
*   **The "Stony" Origins:** The Cree name for this area is *kâ-asinîskâk* ("Stony Creek"). In 1871, **William Bird Jr.** (grandson of a Hudson's Bay Company Governor) built the area's first grist (flour) mill here. He imported the machinery all the way from England, but the creek's flow was too inconsistent to keep the mill running, and he abandoned it after just three seasons.
*   **Gopher Hole Mines:** In 1880, entrepreneurs Moore and W. Ross opened Edmonton's first commercial coal mine in this ravine. Miners used a "drift" method, burrowing horizontally into the banks—hence the nickname "gopher holes." They dug about 30 meters deep to pull out coal used to fuel Hudson’s Bay Company steamboats on the river.
*   **Meat Packing & "The Red Creek":** For much of the 20th century, this ravine was a "toxic wasteland." Two major meatpacking plants—**Gainers** and **Vogel's**—operated right on the banks. Before environmental laws existed, they pumped slaughterhouse waste directly into the water. Long-time locals still tell stories of how the creek would literally **run red with blood** on slaughter days.
*   **An Accidental Forest:** This "gross" history actually shaped the trees you see today. Blood and offal are incredibly rich in nitrogen. This massive, accidental dose of fertilizer favored hardy, nitrogen-loving species like the **Manitoba Maple** and **Balsam Poplar**, allowing them to grow with unnatural speed and density. Meanwhile, the pollution suppressed the native **White Spruce** that would normally dominate a boreal forest. The specific mix of lush, opportunistic greenery in the ravine today is a direct ecological fingerprint of its industrial past.
*   **The "Shortest Railway with the Longest Name":** We are riding on the former right-of-way of the **Edmonton, Yukon & Pacific Railway (EY&P)**. Established in 1902 by tycoons Mackenzie and Mann, it was only a few miles long but provided the first rail link between the CP line in Strathcona and the town of Edmonton across the river.
*   **Wooden Trestles:** The massive timber trestle bridges we cross (like the one at 76th Avenue) are original designs from the early 1900s. They are among the last standing symbols of the city’s early industrial rail history.
*   **The Peace Mile:** Look for the large boulder monuments marking the "Peace Mile" near the Mill Creek pool. Dedicated in **1986** to mark the **UN International Year of Peace**, this measured section of the trail honors world leaders in their pursuit of global harmony. It’s a fitting symbol for a ravine that was saved from industrial destruction by the peaceful but persistent protests of its neighbors.
*   **The Great Freeway Battle:** In 1963, the **METS (Metropolitan Edmonton Transportation Study)** plan proposed turning this ravine into a four-lane freeway to connect the suburbs to downtown. Residents fought for a decade to save it. The turning point was a 1975 standoff where local mothers famously stood in front of bulldozers to halt construction.

## 3. Henrietta Muir Edwards Park
*   **The Legal Researcher:** Named after one of the "Famous Five" women who led the 1929 **Persons Case**. While Nellie McClung was the speaker, Henrietta was the group's "legal brain." Without any formal law degree, she became a self-taught expert and wrote *The Legal Status of Women in Alberta* in 1921.
*   **A Life of Service:** She co-founded the **Victorian Order of Nurses (VON)** in 1897 and the National Council of Women of Canada. She was so dedicated to legal reform that she was still working on handbooks for women's rights at age 80.
*   **The Persons Case:** Remind riders that until 1929, the Supreme Court of Canada ruled that women were not "qualified persons" under the law and therefore could not sit in the Senate. Henrietta and her colleagues took the fight all the way to the Privy Council in England to win the case.
*   **Nuance & Controversy:** It is important to acknowledge that the Famous Five's legacy is complex. Like many reformers of their era, they held views that are considered harmful today. They were vocal supporters of **eugenics**—a pseudoscience aimed at "improving" the human race through selective breeding. Their advocacy led to Alberta's **Sexual Sterilization Act (1928)**, which resulted in the forced sterilization of nearly 3,000 people, disproportionately targeting Indigenous women and those living in poverty. While we celebrate their democratic victories, we must also acknowledge the harm caused by these ideologies.

## 4. Rafter's Landing & The Edmonton Riverboat
*   **A "Lard-Slicked" Beginning:** The 210-tonne steel paddlewheeler was launched on **May 4, 1995**. To get it into the water at Whitemud Creek, crews used wooden ramps made slick with massive amounts of **lard**.
*   **The Queen of the Sandbars:** Originally named the **Edmonton Queen**, the boat has a "sordid" history with the North Saskatchewan River's shifting silt. The river's name comes from the Cree word *kisiskâciwan* ("swift current"), and its shallow, fast-moving waters have claimed many ships.
*   **The 5:00 AM Rescue:** In July 2019, the boat became lodged on a sandbar near the "Accidental Beach." **300 passengers** were stranded overnight and had to be evacuated by the Fire Department via shuttle boats in a rescue that lasted until dawn.
*   **Impaled by the Dock:** In 2020, a sudden 3-meter surge in river volume lifted the boat so high that when the water receded, the hull was **punctured and impaled** by the very steel pilings meant to keep it safe.
*   **John Walter’s Legacy:** This isn't Edmonton's first riverboat. In the early 1900s, local entrepreneur **John Walter** built the *City of Edmonton* and the *Strathcona*. Both eventually met their ends trapped in the river's silt, proving that the North Saskatchewan is a difficult mistress for even the most experienced captains.

## 5. Snack Break: Muttart Conservatory
*   **Gallagher Park:** This park is named after **Cornelius Gallagher**, Edmonton’s third mayor and a meat-packing tycoon. Before 1915, this was a smoke-filled industrial hub with brickyards and packing plants. 
*   **The Bricks that Built the City:** The **Hardstone Brick Company** operated right here from 1907 to 1915. Their "sand-lime" bricks were used to build the Alberta Legislature. You can still see "The Camel Humps" nearby—overgrown mounds of clay waste left behind by the brickyards.
*   **The 1915 Flood:** On June 28, 1915, the river rose a staggering **13.73 meters** (nearly 45 feet). It wiped out the industries here, including John Walter’s lumber mill. To save the Low Level Bridge, the railway parked heavy gravel trains on the tracks to keep it from being swept away by floating houses!
*   **Modernist Pyramids:** Designed by **Peter Hemingway** in 1976. He chose the pyramid shape because it provided a bold, iconic silhouette that wouldn't be dwarfed by the massive river valley. 
*   **The "Fifth" Pyramid:** Most people only count the four biomes, but there is a fifth smaller glass pyramid serving as the skylight for the central foyer. There is also a solid black granite "art pyramid" in the plaza that maps out the microscopic veins of an aspen leaf.

## 6. Nellie McClung Park
*   **The Postcard View:** This is the "money shot" for your group. You can see the Fairmont Hotel Macdonald (built in 1915), the new Walterdale Bridge, and the downtown skyline all in one frame.
*   **The Mock Parliament:** In 1914, Nellie McClung staged a famous "Mock Parliament" at the Walker Theatre. She played the Premier and debated whether *men* should be allowed to vote, using the same condescending arguments men used against women: "Nice men don't want the vote... it would only lead to divorce and unsettle them."
*   **Political Trailblazer:** After winning the right for women to vote, Nellie moved to Edmonton and was elected as an MLA for the city in 1921. She lived just a few blocks from this park during her time in office.
*   **The Black Candle & Exclusion:** It is also important to note that the suffrage movement of this era was often exclusionary. **Emily Murphy**, a close colleague of McClung, authored a notoriously racist book called *The Black Candle* (1922) which blamed racialized immigrants for social ills. While McClung and Murphy fought for the vote, they primarily sought it for white, British-descended women. Indigenous women, for example, did not receive the federal right to vote until 1960. Today, we recognize these as significant moral failings even as we acknowledge the democratic doors they opened.

## 7. Historic Old Strathcona
*   **The Great Rivalry:** Strathcona was the "City of South Edmonton" and a fierce rival to Edmonton. When they amalgamated in 1912, the deal included a promise that the University of Alberta and the main CP rail yards would stay on the south side to protect their economy.
*   **The Strathcona Hotel:** Built in 1891, it is the oldest wood-frame commercial building in the area. During **Prohibition (1918–1924)**, the hotel couldn't sell liquor and went bust. The Presbyterian Church bought it and turned it into the **Westminster Ladies College**! It only became a hotel again once booze was legal again.
*   **Old Strathcona Farmers' Market:** Edmonton's first year-round indoor farmers' market operates out of the historic **Edmonton Bus Barns** (originally built for streetcars). In 1988, locals successfully protested to stop the building from being sold and turned into a "mini-mall." Today, the market has a strict **"make it, bake it, grow it"** policy—meaning every one of the 130+ vendors is strictly audited to ensure no re-selling or imports occur.
*   **The High Level Bridge Streetcar:** This is the **highest streetcar river crossing in the world**. When it first opened in 1913, the cars actually traveled on the "wrong" side of the bridge. This was a safety feature: if a car broke down 150 feet in the air, passengers would step out onto the center of the bridge rather than into the open air! Today, the line is run by volunteers and features restored cars from as far away as Osaka and Melbourne.
*   **The Only Tunnel:** To get to the bridge, the streetcar passes through a 150-meter tunnel under 109th Street. It is the **only tunnel ever built on a Canadian Pacific Railway mainline in Alberta**.
*   **Whyte Avenue Bylaws:** After devastating fires in other prairie towns, a 1902 bylaw required all new buildings on Whyte Ave to be made of brick or stone. This is why the neighborhood has such a distinct, permanent "Old World" feel compared to other parts of the city.

## 8. Finishing: Return to Pedego
*   **Festival City:** Mention that Strathcona hosts the **Edmonton International Fringe Festival**, the largest and oldest of its kind in North America (and second in the world only to Edinburgh). More than 1,500 performers take over these streets every August.
*   **The Loop Complete:** As we finish, remind riders that they've just traveled through 150 years of history—from a failed flour mill to a high-tech conservatory—all without breaking a sweat on their Pedego e-bikes.
